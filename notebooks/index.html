Executive takeaways

The notebook reads like a venue-economics memo, not just an attendance report.

NBA-affiliated teams average 12,542 attendance, compared with 8,610 for independent teams. The strongest teams combine ownership support, premium inventory, and the ability to shift marquee games into bigger buildings.

Most important pattern: the article is not claiming that independents cannot succeed. It is saying the best independent examples win by building an NBA-like premium stack, especially Seattle and Las Vegas.
01 Ownership landscape

The league’s structure is drifting back toward NBA-linked ownership, but the independent teams still matter.

The article’s premise is visible in the ownership map: the biggest expansion decisions tilt toward NBA owners, while the independent holdouts rely more heavily on venue control and premium inventory. Seattle is the clearest example of an independent that can still monetize like a larger-market club.

04 Premium seats and pricing

Premium inventory is the clearest bridge between demand and revenue.

The article argues that “premium seats” are the real economic engine: club seats, courtside rows, suites, signage, and price ladders together explain why some teams look far more scalable than their raw building size suggests.

Article highlights to remember: the Sky’s 770 premium seats, the Wings’ 270 premium seats, the Aces’ $13,000 premium packages, and the Storm’s premium club and suite stack.
05 Demand spikes

Marquee games, sellouts, and Clark-era demand produce visible spikes in the game log.

A line chart is the best way to show the difference between baseline attendance and the spikes created by records, sellouts, and high-interest matchups. The story is less about a single game and more about the repeated ability to create premium demand moments.

Method notes

Sources, joins, and interpretation

Source set

All charts use the normalized outputs in outputs/wnba_expansion/, especially fact_games.csv, dim_teams.csv, dim_venues.csv, fact_venue_financials.csv, and fact_article_claims.csv, plus the market and ownership summary CSVs created by this build.

Key join logic

The notebook treats dim_teams as the ownership dimension, dim_venues as the capacity dimension, fact_games as the game-level event table, and the metro file as a team-level market bridge rather than a row-level join.

Data discrepancy handled

The notebook uses 7,000 seats as the source of truth for College Park Center. The article’s 6,251-seat figure is preserved in the notes as a source discrepancy.
